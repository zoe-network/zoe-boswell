In 1763, a young Scottish lawyer walked into a London bookshop and met the most celebrated mind in England. For the next twenty-one years, James Boswell did something no one else bothered to do — he showed up to Samuel Johnson's meetings, paid close attention, and wrote down what actually happened. Not summaries. Not impressions. The real thing — who said what, the exact words, the moments everyone else forgot by dinner.

Johnson was already famous. But without Boswell, we'd have lost what he actually said. He produced what is still considered the greatest biography in the English language. The person who writes it down is the one who makes it matter.

Boswell's advantage was simple: he was in the room, he was paying attention, and he controlled the record. Two hundred and sixty years later, most of us have lost all three.

Meetings moved online. The platforms that host them — Meet, Teams, Zoom — now offer to write down what happened for you. Which sounds helpful, until you ask who controls that record. The audio goes to their servers. Their model transcribes it. Their summary is what you get back. You don't see the raw transcript. You don't pick the model. You can't run it against your own context or on an air-gapped network.

Every platform has AI meeting notes now. They work. They're also inconsistent, opaque, and out of your control.

A note on privacy: the concerns are real, and in some jurisdictions the regulations are a moving target. You should absolutely disclose that you're using a tool to transcribe. But you should not assume that everyone else on the call — including the platform itself — is being equally transparent about what they're doing with the audio. Zero trust means no assumption of privacy in a remote meeting. Ever.

Same Idea, Different Century

So we built Boswell.

Boswell captures any audio stream on a Linux desktop — Meet, Teams, Zoom, a phone call on speaker — and transcribes it locally using OpenAI's Whisper. The audio never leaves the machine. After the call, it sends the transcript to a local LLM for structured output: summary, decisions, action items, technical discussion, direct quotes. The default model is IBM Granite running through Ollama, but any model works — local or cloud, your choice.

The real trick isn't the model. It's context. Boswell knows who's on the call, what the account history is, and what you're trying to accomplish — before the first word is spoken. That's what turns a transcript into a field report. Johnson had plenty of people in the room. Only one of them was paying attention on purpose.

Head to Head

We tested it head-to-head against Google Gemini's built-in meeting notes on a recorded call, then had three open-source models judge both outputs blind across six criteria.

CriterionWinner
Transcription accuracyGemini
Raw completenessGemini
ActionabilityBoswell
Customer intel extractionBoswell
Overall value as a deliverableBoswell

Gemini won on transcription accuracy and raw completeness — Google Meet has a platform advantage there, with access to individual audio streams and speaker identity. Boswell won on actionability, customer intel extraction, and overall value as a deliverable.

One judge called Gemini's transcript "a dump requiring filtering" and Boswell's output "ready-to-use."

Hardware

Runs on any modern Linux box. We built and tested it on a Ryzen 9 with an RTX 3070 running Red Hat Enterprise Linux, but capture-only mode works fine on a laptop CPU.
